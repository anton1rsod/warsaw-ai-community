### Domains of hard-won knowledge
**Multi-brand monorepos.** Most teams overengineer monorepos with premature abstraction and shared chaos. The real leverage is strict boundaries, brand-level isolation, and controlled sharing (design system, core libs only). I know this from seeing multi-brand setups collapse under coupling and slow release cycles when ownership is unclear.

**SSR / Core Web Vitals.** Most people treat SSR as a silver bullet, but it often just shifts the bottleneck to server latency and hydration. The real gains come from aggressive caching, streaming, and minimizing client JavaScript — not blindly adopting SSR. I know this because measurable improvements in Core Web Vitals come from architecture and delivery strategy, not framework choice.

**Mentoring frontend devs.** Most mentoring is too theoretical and detached from production reality. What actually works is forcing ownership, code reviews with strict standards, and exposing developers to real incidents early. I know this because developers only level up when they feel the cost of mistakes in real systems, not in isolated exercises.

### Recurring patterns I see
**Reading a codebase / PR.** The first signal of trouble is lack of clear boundaries — components mixing data fetching, state, and presentation in one place. I also look at naming and file structure: if intent isn't obvious in seconds, the system won't scale. Over time, I've learned that messy separation always correlates with fragile features and slow delivery.

**Migrations / SSR adoption.** The key signal is whether the team talks about outcomes (performance, SEO, delivery speed) or just tools (Next.js, SSR). If there's no plan for caching, data strategy, and rollout, the migration will stall or regress. Successful teams treat it as an architecture shift, not a framework swap.

**Multi-brand sharing.** The early warning sign is uncontrolled shared layers — when teams start abstracting too early or pushing brand-specific logic into "shared" code. If ownership and versioning aren't defined upfront, everything becomes tightly coupled. I've seen this consistently lead to blocked releases and inability to evolve brands independently.

## Evaluation posture

### What makes me bullish
Clear, narrow problem with high frequency and obvious pain — something users already hack around or pay for. A team that has actually shipped under constraints, not just built demos, and can speak concretely about delivery, tradeoffs, and failure modes. I look for technical realism: they've thought through data flow, scaling, cost, and operational burden, not just architecture diagrams. Strong signal is a defined first user and a credible path to first 10 customers. If they can explain why existing solutions fail in practice — not in theory — I'm bullish.

### What makes me skeptical
Vague problem statements and generic "AI-powered" positioning with no clear user or willingness-to-pay. Founders who talk about scale before they've shipped anything under real constraints. Architecture theatrics — microservices, Kubernetes, event-driven everything — for a product with zero traction. Demos that look polished but have no real data path, no failure handling, no operational story. No clarity on why existing solutions fail in practice. Hand-wavy go-to-market ("we'll go viral") and no plan for first 10 customers. If they can't answer basic tradeoffs or costs, it's a hard no.

### Patterns of failure I've seen recur
1. **Overengineering before validation.** Teams design complex architectures (microservices, heavy abstractions) before proving real user demand. This slows iteration, increases cost, and makes simple changes expensive, killing momentum early.
2. **No clear ownership or boundaries.** As teams scale, lack of defined ownership (code, domains, decisions) leads to shared chaos. Everything becomes coupled, releases get blocked, and velocity collapses under coordination overhead.
3. **Ignoring operational reality.** Products are built as demos, not systems — no monitoring, no failure handling, no cost awareness. Once real users arrive, the system breaks under load or becomes too expensive to run, forcing reactive rewrites.

### Patterns of success I've seen recur
1. **Tight scope with real users early.** Teams pick a narrow, painful problem and get it in front of real users fast. Feedback loops are short, decisions are grounded in usage, and the product evolves based on actual demand, not assumptions.
2. **Clear boundaries and ownership.** Systems are designed with strict domain separation and explicit ownership. This keeps complexity contained, enables parallel work, and allows teams to scale without blocking each other.
3. **Pragmatic engineering with operational focus.** They optimize for delivery and reliability over theoretical purity. Logging, monitoring, cost awareness, and simple deployable architectures are in place from day one, so the system survives real-world usage without constant rewrites.

### My typical first question
Who is the first paying user, what exact pain do they have today, and why aren't existing solutions good enough?
