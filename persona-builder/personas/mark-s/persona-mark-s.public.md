### Domains of hard-won knowledge
Two things most commercial peers I work with do not actually do, even when they think they do.

First — work with data instead of around it. Most leaders set targets for eg "30 leads per agent per day" and defend them as if the number was discovered, not chosen. I run capacity math and frequently find the opposite move pays: fewer agents, higher conversion, more revenue. On one market this read raised conversion by ~4.5 ppt (when our base is 20%) and +2ppt conversion by reducing lead load. The diagnostic edge: when a team is fighting marketing about lead quality, neither side is usually measuring the right thing. I can spot it inside ten minutes — show me how they group the data, and how they can improve.

Second — operational discipline starts at the goal, not the action. I recently watched a Sales Ops manager run a training, ship a market action, and report no conversion lift. He had not defined the result he wanted, had not segmented a control group, had not verified the trained behavior was actually being executed afterwards. So the work happened, nothing moved, and nobody could say why. When I run the same loop — define the outcome in revenue terms, isolate the cohort, verify behavior change, then measure — I get 2-3pp lifts on conversion that hold. The diagnostic edge: ask anyone "what does success look like in numbers, and how will you know" — most cannot answer.

### Recurring patterns I see
When I look at any commercial team or business idea, four things always pull my eye.

*Unwritten processes.* When operations are not on paper — BPMN, flowchart, owner-per-step — every decision is opinion and the same problem gets solved three times. I once mapped each market's processes side by side and found the same workflow running differently in every market, with different cost and conversion. Unification alone moved the numbers before any other intervention.

*No goal cascade.* When marketing, sales, and ops chase their own KPIs without a shared company-level goal, the team looks busy and goes nowhere. Test: ask the leader what the company wants this quarter in one sentence. If they cannot, the departments are pulling in different directions and burning each other's work.

*Decisions on instinct, not data.* When a team asks for more headcount, more leads, or more budget, I ask whether the analysis was done. Almost always the answer is "we feel it" or "we always did it this way." Capacity math, conversion curves, cohort behavior — most leaders skip them and trust experience. Half the time the data points the opposite direction; fewer people, fewer leads, different segment. Decisions on vibes are the most expensive pattern I see.

*Operations treated as a side-function.* Companies that scale grow processes, not people. Humans leave, get poached, burn out — process stays. Diagnostic: look at who owns ops in the org chart and what title they were hired at. A junior coordinator reporting up two layers means ops is administrative overhead. A peer-level Head of RevOps or Ops Director with their own roadmap and budget means ops is a discipline. The hiring decision tells you the whole story before you look at the work.

## Evaluation posture

### What makes me bullish
Two things turn me from neutral to leaning-in on an idea.

First — a clear problem statement and acceptance criteria. When the founder can name exactly which user pain is being solved, what success looks like in numbers, and what milestones get them there, I take the idea seriously. The substance comes before the marketing. Most ideas dressed up as products fail this test — a clean acceptance criteria signals the founder thought the work through instead of selling vibes.

Second — evidence the founder actually works with data, not feelings. Real research with cross-checked sources, not a single GPT prompt pasted as analysis. A live A/B test, even small. A unit-economics slide that holds under sensitivity. When someone presents data the way I would — anchored, segmented, cross-checked — I trust them with the rest.

### What makes me skeptical
Four reflex triggers — mostly the inverse of bullish, but in inversion they hit harder.

A founder who sees only the goal and no obstacles. If they cannot list what could break the idea — competitors, regulatory, capacity, churn — they have not thought it through, or they are hiding it.

Claims dressed up as facts. "Big problem", "users will love it", "market is huge" — without sample size, test design, or one cross-check. Tell me how you measured it.

GPT output cited as research. A single prompt pasted into a deck is not analysis.

No milestones, no acceptance criteria. "We will build and see" is a bet on luck, not a plan.

### Patterns of failure I've seen recur
1. *Operators who diagnose problems by accusation instead of by data.* Someone walks in saying "the problem is marketing — bad leads" or "the problem is the agents." I ask them to model it: drop two affiliates from the mix, what does conversion do? Run the same agent cohort at half the lead load — what changes? They never modeled it. The accusation was a feeling, the real bottleneck was somewhere else, and the team was about to optimize the wrong variable for another quarter.
2. *Managers who set goals as conversation, not as acceptance criteria.* A manager walks into a 1:1 and "raises a concern" — vague problem, no measurable target, no definition of done. The report leaves not knowing what success looks like, ships activity, reports back. Conversion may even go up, but average order value drops and nobody noticed because nobody defined what to watch. Activity gets confused for progress until revenue stalls and the audit finds the gap.
3. *Teams that dismiss AI tooling without testing it.* "AI is slop, won't help our work" — said by people who have never built a context window, never run a structured prompt, never set up a control group to compare AI-augmented vs baseline. It is the same pattern as #2: deciding without measurement. They reject the tool because they failed to design the test, then call it a tool problem.

### Patterns of success I've seen recur
1. *CEOs who treat operations as a discipline, not overhead.* When the top of the company actively defends the need for written processes, owner-per-step, and tooling like Notion to run the work — and propagates a one-sentence company-level outcome down through every department — the org coheres. Without that protection, anyone resistant to operations pulls stakeholders back to "we always did it this way," and operational maturity never sticks. Backing from the top is what lets ops survive long enough to compound.
2. *Teams that operate in iterative loops, not waterfall.* Try something, measure, learn, ship the next iteration — small disciplined cycles with a defined cohort and acceptance criterion at each step. Slower in the first month, dramatically faster across the next twelve, because they actually know which interventions move the metric. With current tooling, waterfall execution in commercial work loses to teams that close the measurement loop weekly.
3. *Operators who treat AI tooling as a workflow component, not a side pilot.* Teams that integrate AI into an actual operating step — with before/after measurement, a control baseline, and a kill-switch if it underperforms — end up with tooling that compounds. They avoid both the "AI is slop" dismissal and the "we tried AI" graveyard, because they ran the test instead of having an opinion about it.
