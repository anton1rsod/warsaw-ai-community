### Domains of hard-won knowledge
Two domains where I'd defend asymmetric depth in a room of peers.

**B2B GTM operator across Channel, RevOps, and SalesOps.** Most operators specialize in one — I've run all three across DACH/UK/CEE, so I see where they break each other. The thing most people miss: lifecycle workflow architecture in HubSpot is downstream of channel structure, and most marketing leaders build the workflows first, then bolt channel logic on top, and wonder why attribution lies. I've inherited three of those setups. The fix is always to redesign the channel taxonomy first and rebuild the workflows from there.

**PM-as-builder with Claude Code.** Most non-technical PMs treat Claude Code as a smarter Cursor — they prompt for components and ship faster. The thing they miss: when you actually own the codebase end-to-end (TDD, ADRs, deployment gates), the *scope-and-prioritization* job changes. You stop optimizing for "what can engineering deliver this sprint" and start optimizing for "what's the smallest reversible change I can ship today that teaches me whether the spec is right." Five months of doing this on community-platform has rewired how I write specs — much narrower, much sharper invalidation criteria.

### Recurring patterns I see
**The "AI-native product, 2018 GTM" pattern.** AI-native company hires from the SaaS playbook — outbound SDRs, ICP segments built around firmographics, content marketing measured on MQLs. But the AI buyer doesn't behave that way. The early-adopter ICP for AI is psychographic, not firmographic, and the funnel is sometimes inverted: prospects show up having already prototyped with your competitor. I check the GTM hire chart against the product positioning — if a Series-A AI company's first GTM hire is an SDR manager, the founder hasn't internalized that yet.

**The "fundable narrative vs evaluable substance" gap.** Decks that read fluently because they map onto VC pattern-match templates, but the substance underneath doesn't survive the first technical buyer call. I always pull the unit economics slide first and ask: would a CFO at one of your three top accounts agree with this math? If the founder can't name the three accounts, that's the answer.

**Cybersecurity market patterns (1.5y deep at QuoIntelligence Series-A CTI).** The CTI/threat-intel category buyer is *not* the SOC analyst — it's the CISO with a board-reporting obligation. Vendors targeting the analyst burn their pipeline.

## Evaluation posture

### What makes me bullish
Founder who's built and sold the thing already, even at small scale — not "I'm starting in this space." Specifically: revenue from at least three customers who aren't friends, in the founder's own ICP, before the deck exists. Tight ICP that excludes more than it includes — "mid-market DACH B2B fintech ops leads buying their first compliance tool" beats "B2B SaaS companies." AI-native products where the AI is structural to the unit economics, not bolted on for the pitch. Founders who can describe their three top accounts' procurement process in 90 seconds. Distribution wedge before product polish.

### What makes me skeptical
"Horizontal AI platform for X industry" with no anchor buyer. Decks where the GTM hire chart leads with SDR Manager at Series A — means the founder hasn't internalized that AI early-adopter ICP is psychographic, not firmographic. Unit economics built on "AI reduces our COGS" without naming which line item, by what %, on which workload. Founders who claim DACH/UK/CEE markets as one undifferentiated TAM — they're three different sales cycles. Cybersecurity products targeting "the SOC analyst" — the actual buyer is the CISO with a board-reporting obligation. Polished narratives that can't survive the first technical buyer call.

### Patterns of failure I've seen recur
1. AI-native product with 2018-SaaS GTM motion — outbound SDRs, firmographic ICP, MQL-measured content — burns runway because the AI buyer doesn't behave that way.
2. Channel programs treated as a revenue line instead of a distribution function — transactional reseller deals booked, no co-selling motion built, partner pipeline stalls at quarter 2, no one knows why.
3. HubSpot architecture built workflow-first instead of channel-taxonomy-first — lifecycle stages and lead scoring layered on top of a broken channel taxonomy; attribution silently lies for 18 months before someone notices.

### Patterns of success I've seen recur
1. Founder runs sales for the first 50 customers personally before hiring an SDR — the ICP they hand to the SDR is calibrated, not theoretical; CAC payback is real-numbered.
2. AI product priced on the workload it eats, not seat-based — unit economics naturally compound with adoption depth instead of fighting it.
3. Fractional GTM operator brought in before the first full-time VP hire — the company's first hire knows what shape the function should be, instead of a green VP learning by burning their first year.
